What is a PC

Understanding the Basics

A PC, or Personal Computer, is a versatile and powerful device used for a wide range of tasks. It consists of various components such as the CPU, GPU, motherboard, RAM, storage, and power supply, all working together to perform computing tasks. PCs are used for gaming, work, education, and entertainment, making them an essential part of modern life.

Different types of PC's

There are different types of PCs designed for specific purposes, such as gaming PCs, workstations, and home PCs. Each type is optimized for its intended use, with gaming PCs focusing on high performance and graphics, workstations on processing power and multitasking, and home PCs on general use and affordability.

Component Focus

Different types of PCs emphasize various components based on their primary use:

  • Gaming PCs: Focus heavily on high-performance GPUs (Graphics Processing Units) to handle demanding graphics and provide smooth gameplay experiences.
  • Workstations: Prioritize powerful CPUs (Central Processing Units) and large amounts of RAM to manage intensive tasks such as video editing, 3D rendering, and software development.
  • Home PCs: Balance between CPU and GPU performance, often with an emphasis on affordability and general-purpose use, such as web browsing, office applications, and media consumption.

Why build a PC?

Customization

Building a PC allows you to customize every aspect of your machine, from the components to the aesthetics. You can choose the best parts that suit your needs and preferences, whether you're a gamer, content creator, or professional.

Performance

Custom-built PCs offer superior performance compared to pre-built systems. You can select high-quality components that deliver exceptional speed, power, and efficiency, tailored to your specific requirements.

Cost-Effectiveness

Building a PC can be more cost-effective than buying a pre-built system. By selecting individual components, you can optimize your budget and allocate resources to the parts that matter most to you, saving money in the long run.

Learning Experience

Building a PC is a valuable learning experience that allows you to understand how computers work, how components interact, and how to troubleshoot issues. It's a hands-on way to gain knowledge and skills in technology and hardware.

Community

PC building is a popular hobby with a vibrant community of enthusiasts, creators, and experts. By building your own PC, you can connect with like-minded individuals, share your experiences, and learn from others in the community.

CPU

What is a CPU (Central processing unit)

The central processing unit (CPU) is the control center of the computer. The main job of the CPU is to interpret, process and execute instructions from the hardware and software programs on the device. The CPU performs arithmetic, logic and other operations to transform data input into more useable information output. CPUs have three main metrics to consider. These being Cores, Threads and clock speeds.

Cores

A core is a single processor within the CPU that can execute instruction by itself. This in toe means that the more cores the CPU has, the more multitasking capabilities the CPU has.

Threads

A thread is a sequence of instructions given to the CPU by programs of applications. The more threads a CPU can execute at once the more task can be completed.

Clock speeds

Clock speeds are the rate at which the CPU can execute instructions. Clock speeds are measured in hertz and indicate how many cycles the a single core can complete per second.

Motherboard

What is a Motherboard

The motherboard is the structural back bone of the computer. the mother board houses all the connections and interfaces for the hardware within the computer. One the key features of a motherboard is many and which types of connections the computer will have. Each motherboard has a chipset produced be either AMD or Intel. The chipset are a collection of electronic components that act as a traffic controller for the flow of data between all connected components.

Compatibility

It is important to know that not all components are compatible with all motherboards. This can come in many forms ranging from incompatible hardware connection types to incompatible software within the components. Depending on the chipset used for the motherboard (either AMD or Intel) the motherboard will be incompatible with CPUs from the other.

GPU

What is a GPU (Graphic processing unit)

The GPU is what calculate and display all the visual from your computer to your monitor. There is a GPU inside every graphics card. The function the GPU is comparable that of the CPU but solely targeted towards the graphics card instead of the computer as a whole. The main difference between a GPU and a CPU is in how many cores they have. The GPU has thousands of smaller cores instead of few big cores. This works because the CPU has less but large calculations and the GPU need to solve many small calculations. Video RAM (VRAM) Like regular RAM, VRAM stores data. The data stored in VRAM is data that is used regularly by the GPU making so that the GPU does not have to do the calculation all over again but can draw on the data stored in the VRAM.

Cooling system

Within a computer every component heats up when it is executing a task. It is important to have a cooling system for the components to avoid thermal throttling, overheating or breakage. The base of the cooling system is the outer fans. These make sure that there is a constant circulation of fresh cooled air into the computer and dispersal of heated air out of the computer. This provides a minor cooling for the components but more importantly gives the other cooling systems new air to work with. Components mostly have two different main cooling systems. Fan cooling systems has a heat sync directly attached to the component which in turn is connected to a fan. When a component heats up, most of the heat is absorbed into the heat sync which in turn is cooled by the fan and cooling the component. The fan then disperses the heat from the component into the air, that is then cycled out through the outer fans. Water cooling uses a radiator, pump and a water block to cycle cooling liquid through the system and cool the components. The heat from the components is absorbed into the cooling liquid within the water block. The heated cooling liquid is then with help of the pump and tubes cycled into the radiator. The radiator uses a series of fans to cool down the liquid again and cycle the cooled liquid back to the water block.

Long term storage

Interfaces

Interfaces are the connecting part between the computer and the hard drive. There are currently 4 types of interfaces used.

Parallel Advanced Technology Attachment (PATA)

First introduced in 1987, the PATA interface can have up to 80GB capacity and transfer data as fast as 133MB/S These are name PATA because they use a parallel ATA interface to connect to the computer. Apart from PATA, these are also called Integrated Drive Electronics (IDE) and Enhanced Integrated Electronics (EIDE) PATA interfaces are made of mechanical moving parts and are capable of transmitting multiple bits of data simultaneously.

Serial Advanced Technology Attachment (SATA)

First introduced in 2000 the SATA interface superseded the PATA interfaces in size, power consumption and better pricing. The mode of connection remains the same as PATA but instead of parallel signaling technology for data transmission they use serial signaling technology meaning that they transfer data one bit at a time. A notable advantage SATA have over PATA interfaces is the transmission of data at a rate of 150-300 MB/S with thinner cables and a longer range.

Small Computer System interface (SCSI)

SCSI is an upgrade over PATA and SATA interfaces for many reasons such as round-the-clock operations, data transfer speed, storage capacity. SCSI use a small computer system interface which is standard for connecting other peripherals such as printers and scanners. They allow connection of other peripherals such as printer, scanners or other hard drives. They are able to transmit data at a rate of 320MB/S and can be connected internally or externally. In modern times, SCSI have been replaced on personal computers by the Universal Serial Bus (USB), meaning that they are no longer used in consumer hardware.

Non-Volatile Memory Express (NVMe)

The NVMe interface is the most modern interface. It is built for high demanding and computing intensive task. Because of the high performance and scalability, they are mostly used in data centers and newer computers. Most newer SSDs are now using NVMe interface. The many advantages over other interfaces are lower power consumption, multiple command queues, reduced latency and input/output overhead and utilization of CPU to full potential. The biggest disadvantage is the steep pricing compared to other interfaces.

Hard disk drives (HDD)

HDDs are most commonly found in older computers. They consist of a moving part that rotates during reading and writing operations. HDDs are slower at reading and writing compared to SSDs but also come at a much lower price. Because of this, they remain popular for storage of files that are less time dependent when reading and writing. The storage capacity ranges from 16GB to 4TB of storage.

Solid state dives (SSD)

SSDs are the latest in hard drive technology. Most newer computers use SSDs instead of HDDs. Unlike the hard drive technologies that came before SSDs they do not use moving parts or magnetism for storing data. Instead they use integrated circuits (ICs) just like third-generation computers. This makes them more durable, faster and less prone to damage and corruption of data. SSDs have a notable advantage of transferring data at a speed of 550 MB/S and allow faster boot times compared to the hard drives that came before them. SSDs like HDDs have a wide range of storage capacity, but they come at a much higher price.

PSU

What is PSU (Power supply unit)

PSUs is the power supply for the computer. The primary function of a PSU is to convert electricity into the specific voltages the computer needs. PSUs comes in many different wattages, so it is important to know how many watts a computer needs when choosing a PSU. The rule of thumb within PSUs is that you need the computers total wattage consumption plus 30%.

Non-modular

A non-modular PSU comes with many different power connections preinstalled within the PSU. This makes them plug and play compared to other types of PSUs but also makes them very rigid. The computer simply can’t have more connection than the PSU allows, and repairs are nearly impossible.

Semi-modular

A semi-modular PSU comes with the most used cables preinstalled with the PSU but also has added connection ports for more connections. This makes them more customizable compared to non-modular PSUs without loosing much of the plug and play factor.

Fully modular

Fully modular PSUs has no preinstalled connection cables but instead has many connection ports. This makes them fully customizable for the computer’s needs but in turn also makes them require some preplanning for the connection cables needed.
